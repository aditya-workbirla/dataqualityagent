Diagnostic and Engineering Knowledge Base: Iron-Chelate Liquid Redox Hydrogen Sulfide Recovery in Viscose Fiber Manufacturing
1. Process Understanding
The manufacturing of viscose staple fiber is a complex, multi-stage chemical process that transforms highly purified plant cellulose into versatile, semi-synthetic textile fibers. The overall process architecture begins with the steeping of wood pulp in caustic soda to form alkali cellulose, followed by shredding, controlled oxidative aging, and xanthation.1 During xanthation, the alkali cellulose is reacted with carbon disulfide (  ) to yield cellulose xanthate, which is subsequently dissolved in a dilute sodium hydroxide solution to form a highly viscous, honey-like fluid known as the viscose dope.3 Following a ripening and filtration phase, this dope is extruded through micro-perforated spinnerets into a highly acidic spin bath composed primarily of sulfuric acid, sodium sulfate, and zinc sulfate.1
It is within this acidic spin bath that the physical and chemical regeneration of the cellulose filament occurs. As the cellulose xanthate coagulates and regenerates into solid cellulose fibers, the chemical reaction violently liberates the previously bound sulfur compounds, outgassing substantial volumes of carbon disulfide (  ) and hydrogen sulfide (  ).3 Because    is a highly toxic, corrosive, and environmentally regulated gas, viscose manufacturing facilities are equipped with robust extraction and recovery systems.6 The dataset provided represents the operational telemetry of the    Retrieval (EDTA Section) of the plant, which utilizes a liquid redox sulfur recovery process based on an iron-chelate catalyst system to capture and convert the fugitive    emissions into benign, elemental sulfur.8
1.1 Process Flow and Operational Sequence Implied by the Variables
The operational sequence mapped by the dataset begins at the primary source of the emissions: the spinning frames. The variables 1st Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A, 2nd Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A, 3rd Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A, and 4th Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A represent the localized gas collection headers, or ring heads, situated directly above or along the acid spin bath enclosures.10 These collection systems are designed to maintain a specific draft pressure or slight vacuum to actively draw the evolved    and    away from the factory floor, ensuring operator safety and preventing fugitive emissions.7
Once captured, the sour gas mixture is pulled through a complex network of ducting by large centrifugal extraction fans, indicated by the variables Blower_Inlet_Pressure_A and Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A. The Blower_Inlet_Pressure_A consistently registers negative values (e.g., -100 to -200 mmwc), reflecting the suction required to pull the gas from the ring heads across the physical distance of the plant.12 The blower imparts kinetic energy to the gas, converting it to the positive static pressure seen in the Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A variable, which is necessary to overcome the downstream aerodynamic resistance of the liquid redox scrubber.13
The pressurized sour gas is then introduced into the primary reaction vessel, the absorption column or scrubber, monitored by the Scrubber_Inlet_Pressure_A and Scrubber_Outlet_Pressure_A tags.12 Inside this vessel, the gas flows upward through a packed bed or spray zone, encountering a counter-current cascade of an aqueous, alkaline solution containing a chelated iron catalyst (typically Ferric-EDTA).8 The liquid redox reaction occurs instantaneously at the gas-liquid interface: the    is absorbed into the solution, dissociates, and is oxidized by the active ferric iron (  ) into solid, elemental sulfur particles, while the iron is reduced to a ferrous state (  ).9 The treated, sweetened gas exits the top of the scrubber, with its final emission quality quantified by the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) variable.8
The "rich" liquid solution, now carrying suspended elemental sulfur and depleted ferrous iron, collects at the bottom of the scrubber. Although not explicitly named in the tag list, process theory dictates that this solution is routed to an oxidizer or regeneration vessel where atmospheric air is sparged through the liquid to re-oxidize the ferrous iron back to the active ferric state, allowing the catalyst solution to be continuously recirculated back to the top of the scrubber.18
Concurrently, a slipstream of this circulating inventory is continuously drawn off to separate and recover the produced solid sulfur. The variables Slurry Mass Flow Rate(kg/hr)(T06)_A and Slurry Density(kg/m3)(T06)_A monitor the behavior of this solid-liquid mixture as it passes through a thickening or settling stage, designated as unit T06.12 Because the elemental sulfur precipitates as highly hydrophilic, colloidal particles, it must be concentrated into a dense slurry before final separation.9 To facilitate complete phase separation, the concentrated slurry is pumped through a heat exchanger to melt the solid sulfur into a distinct liquid sulfur phase. The thermal dynamics of this melting stage are governed by the Heater Steam Pressure(kg/cm2)_A and Heater Temperature(C)_A variables.12
Maintaining the delicate chemical balance of the circulating liquid redox solution requires continuous reactant makeup. The Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A variable tracks the volumetric addition of fresh iron-EDTA catalyst complex, required to replace volume lost to degradation and mechanical carryover.23 Similarly, the Soda Ash Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A is intended to monitor the addition of sodium carbonate (  ), a highly alkaline compound necessary to neutralize the acidic protons generated during the oxidation of   , thereby maintaining the system pH within the optimal operational window.24 The success of this chemical buffering is continuously verified by the pH_A sensor.12
1.2 Upstream and Downstream Relationships Between Variable Groups
The liquid redox sulfur recovery unit operates as a tightly coupled, dynamic hydrodynamic and thermodynamic system where variations in upstream viscose production directly dictate the downstream behavior of the gas treatment facility. If the operational speed of the viscose spinning lines is increased to meet production demands, the total mass flow rate of cellulose xanthate entering the acid spin bath increases proportionately. Consequently, the stoichiometric evolution of    and    gases surges.1 This upstream surge requires the gas extraction blowers to ramp up their volumetric throughput to prevent hazardous gas accumulation in the spinning hall, which manifests as dynamic fluctuations in the 1st through 4th Ring Head Pressure variables and a deeper (more negative) Blower_Inlet_Pressure_A.12
An increased volumetric flow rate of gas entering the scrubber linearly increases the aerodynamic velocity through the packed bed. This results in a higher differential pressure across the column, widening the numerical gap between Scrubber_Inlet_Pressure_A and Scrubber_Outlet_Pressure_A.27 Simultaneously, the increased mass loading of    rapidly depletes the active ferric ions (  ) in the recirculating EDTA solution, creating an immediate localized drop in pH_A due to the rapid generation of protons during the oxidation reaction.9 The automated control systems must respond to this downstream chemical shock by increasing the Soda Ash Dosing Rate to restore alkalinity, while the oxidizer aeration blowers (implied) must increase air flow to accelerate the regeneration of the iron catalyst.18 If the mass loading of    exceeds the available oxygen transfer rate in the regenerator or the available active iron inventory, the reaction becomes reagent-limited, resulting in a breakthrough event where the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) rapidly spikes.28
The relationship between the chemical reaction stage and the physical separation stage is equally critical. The continuous production of solid elemental sulfur in the scrubber acts as a constantly accumulating solid mass within a closed liquid loop. If the downstream T06 thickener and heating stages fail to adequately extract this sulfur, the Slurry Density(kg/m3)(T06)_A will begin to climb beyond optimal ranges (e.g., exceeding 1100 kg/m³).12 As the circulating fluid becomes supersaturated with solid sulfur particles, its kinematic viscosity increases, fundamentally altering the fluid mechanics within the scrubber. A highly viscous, dense slurry will drain more slowly through the packing media, increasing the liquid holdup volume inside the column. This restricts the available cross-sectional area for the upward-flowing gas, further amplifying the scrubber differential pressure and forcing the upstream blower to consume more power, raising the Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A.13 Therefore, a bottleneck in the thermal sulfur melting stage directly translates into an aerodynamic bottleneck in the gas extraction system.
1.3 Normal vs. Abnormal Operations in Context
Holistic process analysis requires an understanding of what constitutes hydrostatic and thermodynamic equilibrium within this specific dataset. Under "normal operation," the ring head pressures should exhibit a stable, steady state consistent with the continuous extrusion of viscose fiber, typically hovering between 1.5 to 2.5 kg/cm² as observed throughout late May and June 2025.12 The extraction blower should maintain a stable negative suction draft (e.g., -100 to -200 mmwc) and a positive discharge pressure sufficient to overcome the scrubber packing without surging. The Slurry Density should remain tightly controlled between 1015 and 1060 kg/m³, indicating that the rate of solid sulfur generation is perfectly matched by the rate of sulfur extraction via the T06 settling and heating system.12 The Heater Temperature(C)_A should remain locked within the optimal sulfur melting window of 115°C to 135°C.22 Crucially, the pH_A should rest comfortably between 8.5 and 9.5, ensuring rapid    dissociation, which keeps the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) suppressed below regulatory limits (typically < 50 ppm).8
Abnormal patterns within the dataset reveal vital, actionable operational narratives for downstream analysts. A profound anomaly occurs between July 3, 2025, and July 7, 2025. During this five-day period, all four Ring Head pressures drop precipitously to near-zero values (e.g., 0.006 to 0.010 kg/cm²), accompanied by a total collapse in the Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A to near-zero or negative values.12 This synchronized collapse across multiple, physically independent variables is the definitive signature of an upstream plant shutdown or a total halt of the viscose spinning lines. The recovery from this event is clearly visible on July 8 and 9, as pressures ramp back up and the liquid redox parameters re-stabilize. For a data scientist training predictive maintenance models, this period must be explicitly flagged as a zero-load state, not an equipment failure, to avoid corrupting algorithms that monitor performance degradation.
Another persistent and glaring abnormality is found in the Soda Ash Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A column, which registers a constant, static negative value (e.g., -1.13 L/hr) across the entire multi-month dataset.12 In the physical reality of fluid transport, a negative volumetric flow rate from a dosing pump into a pressurized chemical system is impossible unless catastrophic check-valve failure is allowing process fluid to flow backward into the chemical storage tank. However, the extreme consistency of this negative value—rarely fluctuating by more than a hundredth of a decimal—indicates a dead-zero calibration drift in the flow transmitter or an inverted 4-20mA signal loop on the instrumentation side.30 Analysts must interpret this variable as a known sensor fault and must not use it as a functional mass-balance variable; instead, the pH_A tag must be relied upon as the sole proxy for system alkalinity.
Furthermore, critical insight can be gleaned by correlating thermal anomalies with emission spikes. On May 29, 2025, the Heater Temperature(C)_A drops significantly to 109.5°C.12 Because elemental sulfur requires temperatures above 115°C to melt into a flowable liquid 22, this temperature drop likely resulted in the solidification of sulfur within the heat exchanger tubes or the downstream decanter, backing up solid sulfur into the circulating inventory. This physical blockage impaired the mass transfer efficiency of the scrubber, leading directly to a massive breakthrough event where the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) spiked to 611 ppm.12 This represents a textbook example of a thermal utility failure cascading into a severe environmental emission violation.

This section summarizes the governing first-principles behavior of the dataset variables, providing the necessary engineering reasoning to support feature engineering, anomaly detection, and data validation.16

2.2 Catalyst Chemistry: The Role of EDTA and Degradation Kinetics
The EDTA(gpl)_A variable monitors the concentration of Ethylene Diamine Tetraacetic Acid, a powerful hexadentate chelating agent.12 The presence of this organic ligand is an absolute physical necessity for the process. In an alkaline environment (pH 8.5 - 9.5), free iron ions are highly insoluble. Without chelation, the ferric ions would instantly react with hydroxyl ions to precipitate out of solution as useless, solid ferric hydroxide (  ), and the ferrous ions would react with the hydrosulfide to precipitate as black ferrous sulfide (  ).23 By enclosing the iron ion within its molecular cage, the EDTA molecule frustrates these precipitation pathways, maintaining the iron in a dissolved, highly reactive state across a broad pH spectrum.18
However, the organic structure of EDTA makes it susceptible to oxidative degradation. During the regeneration phase in the oxidizer, where atmospheric oxygen is sparged into the liquid to convert    back to   , highly reactive intermediate oxygen species, including hydroxyl radicals (  ) and superoxide radicals (  ), are formed.19 These radicals indiscriminately attack the carbon-nitrogen bonds of the EDTA molecule, breaking it down into smaller, ineffective fragments (such as ethylenediaminetriacetic acid or iminodiacetic acid).23
The rate of EDTA degradation follows pseudo-first-order kinetics with respect to the chelate concentration, and is highly sensitive to temperature and radical concentration 23:
  

In the dataset, the Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A primarily reflects the continuous addition of fresh iron-EDTA complex to replace the volume destroyed by this oxidative degradation.12 If the EDTA(gpl)_A concentration drops below a critical stoichiometric threshold relative to the total iron inventory (typically an EDTA:Fe molar ratio below 1.2:1), un-chelated iron will be liberated. This triggers a cascading failure: the free iron forms iron sulfide (  ), turning the circulating liquor pitch black and forming a dense, sticky sludge that rapidly blinds the scrubber packing and overwhelms the T06 thickener.23 For anomaly detection, algorithms should flag sustained downward trends in EDTA(gpl)_A as a leading indicator of impending mechanical fouling and high differential pressure in the scrubber.
2.3 Thermodynamics and Phase Transitions of Elemental Sulfur
The separation and recovery of the solid elemental sulfur generated by the redox reaction is tracked by the Slurry Mass Flow Rate(kg/hr)(T06)_A, Slurry Density(kg/m3)(T06)_A, Heater Steam Pressure(kg/cm2)_A, and Heater Temperature(C)_A variables.12 The physical behavior of sulfur during this thermal separation stage is dictated by extreme, non-linear thermodynamic properties.22
Elemental sulfur at ambient temperatures exists primarily as puckered eight-membered rings (  ) in an orthorhombic crystalline structure. As the slurry is heated in the heat exchanger, the sulfur undergoes a solid-to-liquid phase transition at approximately 115°C.22 At this temperature, it melts into a low-viscosity, straw-yellow liquid that easily decants away from the aqueous EDTA solution due to specific gravity differences. This physical reality explains why the Heater Temperature(C)_A in the dataset operates tightly within the 115°C to 135°C window.12
However, the heating of elemental sulfur involves a profound physical trade-off. If the heater is supplied with excessive thermal energy (indicated by a high Heater Steam Pressure), and the internal temperature of the liquid sulfur breaches approximately 159°C, the substance undergoes a catastrophic   -transition (lambda transition).22 At this critical temperature, the thermal energy causes the    rings to cleave open into diradicals, which immediately polymerize to form incredibly long, entangled chains of polymeric sulfur (  ).
This polymerization causes a sudden, dramatic spike in the dynamic viscosity of the fluid—increasing by a factor of over 10,000 within a span of just a few degrees. The sulfur transforms from a highly fluid liquid into a dark, highly viscous, rubber-like solid. If this occurs inside the shell-and-tube heat exchanger, the equipment will choke instantly, dropping the Slurry Mass Flow Rate(kg/hr)(T06)_A to zero and requiring extensive mechanical drilling or high-pressure hydro-blasting to clear the tubes. Therefore, the control logic governing the Heater Steam Pressure must incorporate strict high-limit clamping to prevent the fluid from ever approaching the 159°C boundary.35
2.4 Fluid Mechanics, Aerodynamics, and Mass Transfer in the Scrubber
The interaction between the gas phase and the liquid phase inside the scrubber dictates the differential pressure of the system, a critical metric for validation and anomaly detection.14 The pressure drop across the scrubber (  ) is a complex function of the gas velocity, the liquid loading rate, the physical characteristics of the packing media, and the physical properties of the liquid.36
The behavior of the    can be understood through generalized pressure drop correlations (GPDC) for packed towers. As the concentration of solid elemental sulfur in the recirculating fluid increases, clearly indicated by a rise in the Slurry Density(kg/m3)(T06)_A, the kinematic viscosity and the specific gravity of the liquid increase. According to fluid mechanical principles, a higher viscosity liquid drains more slowly through the structured or random packing of the scrubber, leading to an increase in liquid holdup—the total volume of liquid trapped within the void spaces of the packing at any given time.36
As liquid holdup increases, the available cross-sectional void area for the upward-flowing gas is reduced. To maintain the same mass flow rate of gas, the actual velocity of the gas through these constricted channels must increase, which exponentially increases the frictional pressure drop (  ).
If the Slurry Density becomes excessively high, the upward shear stress of the high-velocity gas will equal and eventually overcome the downward gravitational force acting on the liquid film. This defines the "loading point" of the scrubber. If pushed beyond this point, the scrubber enters a "flooded" regime.14 During flooding, liquid is physically blown upward and out of the top of the column, gas-liquid mass transfer collapses entirely, and the Scrubber_Inlet_Pressure_A will spike violently, forcing the upstream blower to surge.13 Analysts can utilize the derivative of the scrubber    relative to the Slurry Density to build highly accurate predictive models that warn operators of impending column flooding before a total aerodynamic collapse occurs.

* Function & Signatures: Centrifugal blowers operate by imparting kinetic energy to the gas stream via a rapidly rotating impeller, converting velocity into static pressure within a volute casing. The Blower_Inlet_Pressure_A measures the suction draft pulled by the eye of the impeller, while the Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A measures the positive pressure exerted into the scrubber ducting.12 The 1st through 4th Ring Head Pressure variables measure the specific draft maintained at the individual spinning lines.
* Input-Output Relationships: According to the fan affinity laws, the volumetric flow rate is directly proportional to the rotational speed of the blower, while the pressure developed is proportional to the square of the speed. In a well-tuned system, minor fluctuations in upstream viscose production should be met with smooth, automated adjustments to the blower's variable frequency drive (VFD) or inlet guide vanes, maintaining a highly stable Blower_Inlet_Pressure.
* Common Failure Modes & Data Patterns:

Prolonged surging will result in catastrophic mechanical failure of the blower's thrust bearings.
   * Impeller Fouling: Exhaust gases from the spinning bath frequently carry entrained acid mist, viscose particulates, and condensing sulfur compounds. Over time, this sticky particulate matter can accumulate asymmetrically on the blower impeller blades. 3.3 Slurry Thickening and Transport: The T06 System
Implied Equipment: Gravity Settling Tanks (Thickeners), Rake Mechanisms, Slurry Transfer Pumps.20
The continuous removal of the solid sulfur byproduct is absolutely critical to maintaining the hydraulic stability of the liquid redox solution.9
         * Function & Signatures: The T06 unit represents a gravity thickener or clarifier. The relatively dilute slurry exiting the scrubber and oxidizer is introduced into this large, quiescent tank. According to Stoke's Law, the heavy elemental sulfur particles settle to the conical bottom, forming a dense sludge, while the clear, regenerated Fe-EDTA liquor overflows a weir at the top to be recirculated. The Slurry Density(kg/m3)(T06)_A monitors the concentration of the settled underflow, and the Slurry Mass Flow Rate(kg/hr)(T06)_A tracks the extraction rate of this sludge via centrifugal or progressive cavity slurry pumps.39
         * Common Failure Modes & Data Patterns:

         * Pump Cavitation and Blockage: Extracting highly concentrated sulfur slurry is mechanically abusive. If the Slurry Density climbs excessively high (e.g., >1150 kg/m³), the apparent viscosity of the non-Newtonian fluid increases drastically. In the dataset, cavitation appears as highly erratic, noisy variance in the Slurry Mass Flow Rate, occasionally dropping to zero as the pump loses its prime.39

4.2 Alarm and Trip Philosophies
The protection of personnel, the environment, and capital equipment in a liquid redox plant requires a sophisticated, cascading alarm philosophy that accounts for the coupled physical dependencies detailed in Section 2.
               1. The Environmental Breakthrough Hierarchy: A high-high alarm on the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) (e.g., > 100 ppm) is a critical environmental event.28 The PLC control logic must autonomously execute a root-cause diagnostic tree. It must first interrogate the pH_A variable; if the pH has crashed below 7.5, the failure is chemical (a failure in the Soda Ash Dosing system).9 If the pH remains within the normal alkaline window, the logic must immediately check the scrubber differential pressure. An exceptionally high    implicates mechanical flooding, while a drastically low    implies a failure of the main liquid recirculation pumps (loss of mass transfer area).
               2. The Thermal Polymerization Override: Because the polymerization of liquid sulfur at 159°C is instantaneous, catastrophic, and irreversible within the confines of a heat exchanger 22, the Heater Temperature loop must incorporate a hard, un-bypassable hardware trip. If the exiting fluid temperature touches 145°C, the Heater Steam Pressure control valve must automatically fail-closed and vent, irrespective of the slurry flow rate or the demands of the primary PID loop, to prevent the total loss of the heat exchanger asset.35
               3. Aerodynamic Stall Protection: Centrifugal blowers handling acidic, particulate-laden gases are highly susceptible to surging if downstream resistance spikes.13 The control system must monitor the variance in Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A. If the pressure oscillates rapidly by more than    of the setpoint over a continuous 60-second rolling window, it is a definitive aerodynamic signature of surging. The system must trigger an immediate operator alarm to open an anti-surge bypass valve or reduce the VFD speed, as prolonged surging will explosively shatter the blower's thrust bearings.
4.3 Cautionary Guidance for Data Analysts
For downstream analysts, data scientists, and engineers utilizing this dataset for machine learning, predictive maintenance modeling, or process optimization, strict adherence to the following "do not over-interpret" boundaries is required:
                  * Handling Instrumentation Blind Spots: Do not incorporate the Soda Ash Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A into predictive mass-balance or stoichiometric models until the physical sensor offset is corrected by the instrumentation team. Training machine learning algorithms on this constant negative value will result in the model learning mathematically false correlations.12 Instead, this column should be transformed into a continuous Boolean flag representing a "known sensor fault," and analysts should rely entirely on the mathematical derivative of the pH_A changes to infer the actual rate of alkalinity addition and depletion.
                  * Decoupling Mechanical Transients from Chemical Inertia: Analysts must recognize the vast difference in temporal response times between aerodynamic variables and chemical variables. Gas phase parameters, such as Scrubber_Inlet_Pressure and Blower Discharge Pressure, react instantaneously at the speed of sound to changes in upstream load or valve positioning.14 Conversely, liquid phase chemical variables, such as pH_A and EDTA(gpl)_A, possess massive thermal and volumetric inertia because the system contains tens of thousands of liters of liquid inventory.19 Do not attempt to correlate second-by-second high-frequency noise in the aerodynamic data with shifts in the chemical concentration data. For feature engineering, use hourly or daily rolling averages and exponentially weighted moving averages (EWMA) for all chemical variables, while preserving high-resolution time-series data for the mechanical pressure tags.
                  * Delineating Process States vs. Degradation Faults: Predictive maintenance algorithms are highly susceptible to data corruption if they cannot distinguish between an intentional operational state change and a mechanical failure.44 For example, the severe drop in all variables on July 3, 2025, represents a coordinated, intentional plant shutdown (a zero-load state), not a simultaneous catastrophic failure of multiple independent equipment classes.12 Models must be explicitly programmed with threshold logic to recognize these "zero-flow" states and automatically pause or mask the calculation of degradation, fouling, or catalyst consumption metrics during these periods to ensure the predictive timelines remain accurate and actionable.
