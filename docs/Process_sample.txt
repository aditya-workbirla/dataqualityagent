1. Process Understanding
The manufacturing of viscose staple fiber is a complex, multi-stage chemical process that transforms highly purified plant cellulose into versatile, semi-synthetic textile fibers. The overall process architecture begins with the steeping of wood pulp in caustic soda to form alkali cellulose, followed by shredding, controlled oxidative aging, and xanthation.1 During xanthation, the alkali cellulose is reacted with carbon disulfide (  ) to yield cellulose xanthate, which is subsequently dissolved in a dilute sodium hydroxide solution to form a highly viscous, honey-like fluid known as the viscose dope.3 Following a ripening and filtration phase, this dope is extruded through micro-perforated spinnerets into a highly acidic spin bath composed primarily of sulfuric acid, sodium sulfate, and zinc sulfate.1
It is within this acidic spin bath that the physical and chemical regeneration of the cellulose filament occurs. As the cellulose xanthate coagulates and regenerates into solid cellulose fibers, the chemical reaction violently liberates the previously bound sulfur compounds, outgassing substantial volumes of carbon disulfide (  ) and hydrogen sulfide (  ).3 Because    is a highly toxic, corrosive, and environmentally regulated gas, viscose manufacturing facilities are equipped with robust extraction and recovery systems.6 The dataset provided represents the operational telemetry of the    Retrieval (EDTA Section) of the plant, which utilizes a liquid redox sulfur recovery process based on an iron-chelate catalyst system to capture and convert the fugitive    emissions into benign, elemental sulfur.8
1.1 Process Flow and Operational Sequence Implied by the Variables
The operational sequence mapped by the dataset begins at the primary source of the emissions: the spinning frames. The variables 1st Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A, 2nd Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A, 3rd Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A, and 4th Ring Head Pressure(kg/cm2)_A represent the localized gas collection headers, or ring heads, situated directly above or along the acid spin bath enclosures.10 These collection systems are designed to maintain a specific draft pressure or slight vacuum to actively draw the evolved    and    away from the factory floor, ensuring operator safety and preventing fugitive emissions.7
Once captured, the sour gas mixture is pulled through a complex network of ducting by large centrifugal extraction fans, indicated by the variables Blower_Inlet_Pressure_A and Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A. The Blower_Inlet_Pressure_A consistently registers negative values (e.g., -100 to -200 mmwc), reflecting the suction required to pull the gas from the ring heads across the physical distance of the plant.12 The blower imparts kinetic energy to the gas, converting it to the positive static pressure seen in the Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A variable, which is necessary to overcome the downstream aerodynamic resistance of the liquid redox scrubber.13
The pressurized sour gas is then introduced into the primary reaction vessel, the absorption column or scrubber, monitored by the Scrubber_Inlet_Pressure_A and Scrubber_Outlet_Pressure_A tags.12 Inside this vessel, the gas flows upward through a packed bed or spray zone, encountering a counter-current cascade of an aqueous, alkaline solution containing a chelated iron catalyst (typically Ferric-EDTA).8 The liquid redox reaction occurs instantaneously at the gas-liquid interface: the    is absorbed into the solution, dissociates, and is oxidized by the active ferric iron (  ) into solid, elemental sulfur particles, while the iron is reduced to a ferrous state (  ).9 The treated, sweetened gas exits the top of the scrubber, with its final emission quality quantified by the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) variable.8
The "rich" liquid solution, now carrying suspended elemental sulfur and depleted ferrous iron, collects at the bottom of the scrubber. Although not explicitly named in the tag list, process theory dictates that this solution is routed to an oxidizer or regeneration vessel where atmospheric air is sparged through the liquid to re-oxidize the ferrous iron back to the active ferric state, allowing the catalyst solution to be continuously recirculated back to the top of the scrubber.18
Concurrently, a slipstream of this circulating inventory is continuously drawn off to separate and recover the produced solid sulfur. The variables Slurry Mass Flow Rate(kg/hr)(T06)_A and Slurry Density(kg/m3)(T06)_A monitor the behavior of this solid-liquid mixture as it passes through a thickening or settling stage, designated as unit T06.12 Because the elemental sulfur precipitates as highly hydrophilic, colloidal particles, it must be concentrated into a dense slurry before final separation.9 To facilitate complete phase separation, the concentrated slurry is pumped through a heat exchanger to melt the solid sulfur into a distinct liquid sulfur phase. The thermal dynamics of this melting stage are governed by the Heater Steam Pressure(kg/cm2)_A and Heater Temperature(C)_A variables.12
Maintaining the delicate chemical balance of the circulating liquid redox solution requires continuous reactant makeup. The Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A variable tracks the volumetric addition of fresh iron-EDTA catalyst complex, required to replace volume lost to degradation and mechanical carryover.23 Similarly, the Soda Ash Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A is intended to monitor the addition of sodium carbonate (  ), a highly alkaline compound necessary to neutralize the acidic protons generated during the oxidation of   , thereby maintaining the system pH within the optimal operational window.24 The success of this chemical buffering is continuously verified by the pH_A sensor.12
1.2 Upstream and Downstream Relationships Between Variable Groups
The liquid redox sulfur recovery unit operates as a tightly coupled, dynamic hydrodynamic and thermodynamic system where variations in upstream viscose production directly dictate the downstream behavior of the gas treatment facility. If the operational speed of the viscose spinning lines is increased to meet production demands, the total mass flow rate of cellulose xanthate entering the acid spin bath increases proportionately. Consequently, the stoichiometric evolution of    and    gases surges.1 This upstream surge requires the gas extraction blowers to ramp up their volumetric throughput to prevent hazardous gas accumulation in the spinning hall, which manifests as dynamic fluctuations in the 1st through 4th Ring Head Pressure variables and a deeper (more negative) Blower_Inlet_Pressure_A.12
An increased volumetric flow rate of gas entering the scrubber linearly increases the aerodynamic velocity through the packed bed. This results in a higher differential pressure across the column, widening the numerical gap between Scrubber_Inlet_Pressure_A and Scrubber_Outlet_Pressure_A.27 Simultaneously, the increased mass loading of    rapidly depletes the active ferric ions (  ) in the recirculating EDTA solution, creating an immediate localized drop in pH_A due to the rapid generation of protons during the oxidation reaction.9 The automated control systems must respond to this downstream chemical shock by increasing the Soda Ash Dosing Rate to restore alkalinity, while the oxidizer aeration blowers (implied) must increase air flow to accelerate the regeneration of the iron catalyst.18 If the mass loading of    exceeds the available oxygen transfer rate in the regenerator or the available active iron inventory, the reaction becomes reagent-limited, resulting in a breakthrough event where the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) rapidly spikes.28
The relationship between the chemical reaction stage and the physical separation stage is equally critical. The continuous production of solid elemental sulfur in the scrubber acts as a constantly accumulating solid mass within a closed liquid loop. If the downstream T06 thickener and heating stages fail to adequately extract this sulfur, the Slurry Density(kg/m3)(T06)_A will begin to climb beyond optimal ranges (e.g., exceeding 1100 kg/m³).12 As the circulating fluid becomes supersaturated with solid sulfur particles, its kinematic viscosity increases, fundamentally altering the fluid mechanics within the scrubber. A highly viscous, dense slurry will drain more slowly through the packing media, increasing the liquid holdup volume inside the column. This restricts the available cross-sectional area for the upward-flowing gas, further amplifying the scrubber differential pressure and forcing the upstream blower to consume more power, raising the Blower Discharge Pressure(mmwc)_A.13 Therefore, a bottleneck in the thermal sulfur melting stage directly translates into an aerodynamic bottleneck in the gas extraction system.
1.3 Normal vs. Abnormal Operations in Context
Holistic process analysis requires an understanding of what constitutes hydrostatic and thermodynamic equilibrium within this specific dataset. Under "normal operation," the ring head pressures should exhibit a stable, steady state consistent with the continuous extrusion of viscose fiber, typically hovering between 1.5 to 2.5 kg/cm² as observed throughout late May and June 2025.12 The extraction blower should maintain a stable negative suction draft (e.g., -100 to -200 mmwc) and a positive discharge pressure sufficient to overcome the scrubber packing without surging. The Slurry Density should remain tightly controlled between 1015 and 1060 kg/m³, indicating that the rate of solid sulfur generation is perfectly matched by the rate of sulfur extraction via the T06 settling and heating system.12 The Heater Temperature(C)_A should remain locked within the optimal sulfur melting window of 115°C to 135°C.22 Crucially, the pH_A should rest comfortably between 8.5 and 9.5, ensuring rapid    dissociation, which keeps the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) suppressed below regulatory limits (typically < 50 ppm).8
Abnormal patterns within the dataset reveal vital, actionable operational narratives for downstream analysts. A profound anomaly occurs between July 3, 2025, and July 7, 2025. During this five-day period, all four Ring Head pressures drop precipitously to near-zero values (e.g., 0.006 to 0.010 kg/cm²), accompanied by a total collapse in the Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A to near-zero or negative values.12 This synchronized collapse across multiple, physically independent variables is the definitive signature of an upstream plant shutdown or a total halt of the viscose spinning lines. The recovery from this event is clearly visible on July 8 and 9, as pressures ramp back up and the liquid redox parameters re-stabilize. For a data scientist training predictive maintenance models, this period must be explicitly flagged as a zero-load state, not an equipment failure, to avoid corrupting algorithms that monitor performance degradation.
Another persistent and glaring abnormality is found in the Soda Ash Dosing Rate(L/hr)_A column, which registers a constant, static negative value (e.g., -1.13 L/hr) across the entire multi-month dataset.12 In the physical reality of fluid transport, a negative volumetric flow rate from a dosing pump into a pressurized chemical system is impossible unless catastrophic check-valve failure is allowing process fluid to flow backward into the chemical storage tank. However, the extreme consistency of this negative value—rarely fluctuating by more than a hundredth of a decimal—indicates a dead-zero calibration drift in the flow transmitter or an inverted 4-20mA signal loop on the instrumentation side.30 Analysts must interpret this variable as a known sensor fault and must not use it as a functional mass-balance variable; instead, the pH_A tag must be relied upon as the sole proxy for system alkalinity.
Furthermore, critical insight can be gleaned by correlating thermal anomalies with emission spikes. On May 29, 2025, the Heater Temperature(C)_A drops significantly to 109.5°C.12 Because elemental sulfur requires temperatures above 115°C to melt into a flowable liquid 22, this temperature drop likely resulted in the solidification of sulfur within the heat exchanger tubes or the downstream decanter, backing up solid sulfur into the circulating inventory. This physical blockage impaired the mass transfer efficiency of the scrubber, leading directly to a massive breakthrough event where the Outlet H2S Concentration(ppm) spiked to 611 ppm.12 This represents a textbook example of a thermal utility failure cascading into a severe environmental emission violation.
